Parts of the Eye

  • Cornea - clear surface that protects the eye and helps focus light
  • Pupil - dark opening that lets light enter the eye
  • Iris - colored ring around the pupil
  • Lens - focuses light onto the retina
  • Retina - light-sensitive surface at the back of the eye
  • Fovea - small region used for high-detail vision

These notes add detail for students who want more anatomy than the visible slide provides. The goal is not memorizing biology; it is understanding how the eye supports visual sampling, detailed inspection, and peripheral awareness.

Cornea: The cornea is the clear, curved front surface of the eye. It provides much of the eye’s focusing power by bending incoming light before it reaches the pupil and lens. Because it is exposed at the front of the eye, it also helps protect the more delicate internal structures.

Pupil: The pupil is the dark opening that lets light enter the eye. It gets larger in dim environments and smaller in bright environments, which helps regulate how much light reaches the retina. The pupil is not a physical object by itself; it is the opening created by the surrounding iris.

Iris: The iris is the colored ring of muscle around the pupil. It controls pupil size by constricting or dilating the pupil in response to light and other physiological conditions. This adjustment helps balance visibility and protection: too little light makes details hard to see, while too much light can overwhelm the visual system.

Lens: The lens sits behind the pupil and changes shape to focus light onto the retina. This focusing process, called accommodation, helps the eye keep objects clear at different distances. The key takeaway for HCI is that seeing detail depends on both where the eye is directed and whether the visual information can be focused clearly.

Retina: The retina is the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. It converts light into neural signals that travel toward the brain. The retina is not uniform: peripheral regions are useful for motion, broad layout, and context, while central regions support higher-detail inspection. This is why a user can notice something in the periphery but still need to move their eyes to read a value, inspect a label, or verify a small control state.

Fovea: The fovea is a small central region of the retina with very high visual acuity. Fine detail, such as text, small icons, numeric values, and subtle visual differences, is usually processed best when it falls near the fovea. Eye movements are therefore a sampling strategy: users move the fovea from one information source to another when they need detailed visual information.

Rods, Cones, and Foveal Vision

  • Cones - color and fine detail
  • Rods - motion and low-light sensitivity
  • Fovea - narrow zone of sharp vision
  • Implication - eyes move to inspect detail

This slide explains why the fovea is a small visual zone rather than a large area of equally sharp vision. The figure plots receptor density across visual angle, with zero degrees marking the fovea.

Cones are densely packed near the fovea. They support high-acuity detail and color vision, which is why reading text, distinguishing small symbols, and inspecting fine visual differences work best when the target is near the center of gaze.

Rods are more common outside the fovea. They are sensitive in low light and useful for detecting motion, change, and broad spatial layout. Rods help peripheral vision notice that something happened, but they do not support the same fine detail as foveal cone vision.

The foveal region covers only a very small part of the visual field, roughly the central 1-2 degrees for the sharpest detail. As information falls farther from the fovea, detailed recognition drops quickly even though peripheral awareness remains useful.

The practical implication is that people move their eyes to inspect details. Peripheral vision helps guide where to look next, and saccades reposition the fovea onto the information source that needs closer inspection.

High-Level Terminology

  • Gaze - where the eyes are directed
  • Attention - selected information for processing
  • Gaze and attention often overlap
  • Gaze does not prove comprehension or intent
  • In task work, people often look where useful information is expected

Make the caution early: eye tracking estimates gaze behavior. It does not directly measure thought, awareness, or understanding.

Source: Land and Tatler, Looking and Acting: Vision and Eye Movements in Natural Behaviour, Oxford University Press, 2009, Chs. 1 and 3.

Foveal Sampling

  • High-detail vision is concentrated near the fovea
  • Peripheral vision supports context, motion, and rough location
  • Small labels, values, and controls usually require fixation
  • Visual displays should make important information easy to sample

Connect this to dashboards and control panels: small warnings or values may be visible, but they may not be readable without direct gaze.

Source: Land and Tatler, Looking and Acting: Vision and Eye Movements in Natural Behaviour, Oxford University Press, 2009, Ch. 2.

This overview introduces four movement patterns before the lesson moves into individual definitions. The images are direct examples from the iMotions eye movements explainer.

Saccades are rapid jumps that move gaze from one location to another. Pursuit movements follow a moving target more smoothly. Vergence aligns the two eyes for depth by moving them in opposite directions. Stabilizing movements, such as the vestibulo-ocular reflex, help keep a target stable on the retina while the head or body moves.

Fixations and Saccades

  • Fixation - relative gaze stability for information uptake
  • Saccade - rapid shift to a new location
  • Scanpath - ordered sequence of fixations and saccades
  • HCI use: compare the scanpath with the task’s expected information sequence

Fixations are not perfectly still; they are algorithm-defined events. For HCI, the useful idea is that fixations mark likely information samples.

Source: Huang, Doebler, and Mertins, “Short-time AOIs-based representative scanpath identification and scanpath aggregation,” Behavior Research Methods 56 (2024), Fig. 1, p. 6053, doi:10.3758/s13428-023-02332-w.

Pursuit, Stabilization, Vergence

  • Smooth pursuit - tracking a moving target
  • Stabilizing movements - keeping gaze stable during head or scene motion
  • Vergence - aligning both eyes for depth
  • These matter in mobile, vehicle, AR/VR, and physical work studies

Most HCI analyses emphasize fixations and saccades, but these other movements matter when the environment or target is moving.

Source: Land and Tatler, Looking and Acting: Vision and Eye Movements in Natural Behaviour, Oxford University Press, 2009, Ch. 2.

Bottom-Up and Top-Down

  • Bottom-up - attention is pulled by visual features
  • Top-down - attention is guided by goals and expectations
  • Salient features can help alerts stand out
  • Task goals guide where people look for needed information

Use a simple contrast: a flashing alarm can pull attention, but an operator’s task determines whether they next inspect the trend, the setpoint, or the control state.

Source: Land and Tatler, Looking and Acting: Vision and Eye Movements in Natural Behaviour, Oxford University Press, 2009, Ch. 3.

Attention: Bottom-Up

Bottom-up attention refers to attention that is driven by the visual stimulus itself, rather than by the viewer’s current goal. If top-down attention is “I am looking for the pressure gauge,” bottom-up attention is “that flashing red light pulled my eyes before I decided to look there.”

A visual feature becomes salient when it stands out from its surroundings. A red object among gray objects, a flashing warning icon on a static dashboard, a tilted line among vertical lines, or a bright object in a dark scene can all attract attention because they are locally distinctive. Classic models of visual saliency emphasize low-level features such as intensity, color, and orientation, which are combined into a saliency map that predicts conspicuous locations in the scene.

Important bottom-up features include:

Color contrast. A red, yellow, or blue element can attract attention when the surrounding display is mostly neutral. The key is not the color alone, but the contrast between the item and its context. A red warning light on a gray HMI panel is salient; a red light in a display full of red objects is much less salient.

Brightness and luminance contrast. Bright regions against a dark background, or dark regions against a bright background, tend to attract attention. This is why high-contrast labels, alarms, borders, and highlights are visually powerful.

Motion, flicker, and animation. Movement is one of the strongest ways to attract attention. In an interface, blinking icons, scrolling banners, animated notifications, and moving objects can pull gaze even when the user is trying to focus elsewhere.

Sudden onset. A newly appearing object often captures attention because the visual system treats abrupt changes as potentially important. In HCI, pop-ups, alerts, badges, and appearing or disappearing indicators use this principle.

Orientation and shape contrast. A tilted object among vertical objects, or a diamond among circles, can stand out because it differs from the local pattern. Visual search research often uses these “singleton” displays to study how a unique feature captures attention.

Size and scale. Large objects, large labels, thick borders, and oversized icons tend to attract attention because they occupy more visual area and often imply importance.

Local detail or clutter. Dense texture, sharp edges, high spatial detail, and complex regions can attract gaze because they contain more visual information. However, clutter can also make search harder by creating too many competing points of interest.

Faces, eyes, text, and symbols. These are not purely low-level features, but they are extremely strong attention attractors in real scenes. People tend to look at faces, eyes, words, arrows, warning symbols, and other meaningful objects. This is why real-world saliency is not purely bottom-up; meaning and learned expectations also matter.

In visual search, bottom-up salience can either support or disrupt the user’s task. It supports the task when the salient item is important, such as a true alarm, critical warning, or active system state. It disrupts the task when an irrelevant object is more visually salient than the information the user actually needs. Theeuwes’s attentional-capture work shows that salient singletons can capture attention even when they are not the target, and later work describes these singletons as producing an “attend-to-me” signal that can sometimes be suppressed but often competes with goal-directed control.

Attention: Top Down

This slide shows Yarbus’ classic demonstration that gaze is shaped by top-down goals. The same image, Repin’s An Unexpected Visitor, was inspected under different questions or viewing goals. The scan paths changed depending on what information the viewer needed, so the eye movements cannot be explained by image salience alone.

The important teaching point is that “where people look” depends on the task they are trying to solve. If the viewer is judging ages, faces become central. If the viewer is remembering clothing, gaze moves over bodies and garments. If the viewer is inferring relationships and absence, gaze moves among faces and social cues.

Section 2.2: Eye Tracking

  • How eye trackers work
  • Area of Interest

How Eye Trackers Work

  • Capture an image of the eye
  • Detect the pupil and/or corneal reflection
  • Estimate gaze direction
  • Calibrate using known points
  • Map gaze to a screen, scene, or virtual environment

Keep this as a measurement chain. Gaze data are processed estimates, so calibration and context matter.

Area of Interest

  • AOI - Area of Interest
  • AOIs define task-relevant regions for analysis
  • Static AOIs: buttons, charts, panels, labels
  • Dynamic AOIs: tools, vehicles, people, moving targets
  • Good AOIs come from the task question

AOIs are analysis choices, not natural facts. They should represent information sources that matter to the task.

Source: Li et al., “Using Eye Tracking to Examine Expert-Novice Differences During Simulated Surgical Training,” Computers in Human Behavior 144 (2023), Fig. 4, PDF p. 5, doi:10.1016/j.chb.2023.107720.

Visualizing Gaze

  • Saliency or heat map - aggregate spatial concentration
  • Scanpath - ordered fixation sequence
  • AOI network - transitions between regions
  • AOI timeline or trace - where gaze went over time
  • Choose the visualization for the question

Heat maps flatten time. Scanpaths and AOI timelines keep more of the task sequence.

Source: Starke et al., “Workflows and Individual Differences During Visually Guided Routine Tasks in a Road Traffic Management Control Room,” Applied Ergonomics 61 (2017), Fig. 4, p. 84, doi:10.1016/j.apergo.2017.01.006.

Visual Routines

  • Task-directed pattern of looking and attending
  • Used to gather visual information for a goal
  • Can include locating, comparing, monitoring, guiding, and checking
  • Interface layout can support or disrupt the routine

Example: alarm text -> trend -> setpoint -> related equipment -> response -> feedback.

Source: Land and Tatler, Looking and Acting: Vision and Eye Movements in Natural Behaviour, Oxford University Press, 2009.

Comparison

  • Many tasks require comparing two or more information sources
  • Examples: current value vs. target, option A vs. option B, expected vs. observed
  • Gaze may alternate between comparison regions
  • Extra revisits can suggest uncertainty, memory load, or poor grouping

This slide is intentionally simple. The design point is to place comparison information close enough that the visual routine is easy.

Visual Search

  • Context-guided search - look where the object is expected to be
  • Feature-guided search - look for a known visual feature
  • Search is easier when layout and visual coding match expectations
  • Poor layout turns routine search into exploration

Use “find the red warning” for feature-guided search and “the filter should be near the chart” for context-guided search.

Situation Awareness Scan

  • Situation awareness requires scanning critical information sources
  • A scan may include current state, trend, alerts, controls, and feedback
  • Missed regions can indicate risk or a task-design mismatch
  • Overly narrow scanning can miss changes outside the current focus

Use this for dashboards, vehicles, aviation, healthcare, and control rooms. The key question is whether gaze covers the information needed to maintain awareness.

Gaze Phenomena

  • Expertise
  • Quiet eye
  • Attention tunneling
  • Gaze cueing
  • Fatigue effects
  • Gaze bias, comparison, and confidence

Expertise

  • Experts often sample task-relevant information more efficiently
  • Novices may show broader search and more revisits
  • Visual efficiency - less unnecessary looking
  • Information reduction - focusing on cues that matter for the task

Avoid treating expert gaze as a universal template. Expertise effects depend on task, context, and training.

Source: Li et al., “Using Eye Tracking to Examine Expert-Novice Differences During Simulated Surgical Training,” Computers in Human Behavior 144 (2023), Fig. 10, PDF p. 10, doi:10.1016/j.chb.2023.107720.

Quiet Eye

  • Final fixation before a skilled action
  • Often discussed in sports and skilled motor performance
  • Useful idea for timing: where does the performer look before acting?
  • HCI analog: gaze to key feedback or control state before committing

Keep this brief and conceptual. The main takeaway is anticipatory fixation before skilled action.

Attention Tunneling

  • Attention becomes too narrow
  • Important peripheral or secondary information is missed
  • More likely under stress, workload, time pressure, or alarms
  • Relevant to driving, aviation, healthcare, and industrial displays

Link this to situation awareness: the user may be looking intensely, but not broadly enough.

Gaze Cueing

  • People use others’ gaze direction as an attention cue
  • Gaze can guide joint attention in teams
  • Useful in training, collaboration, telepresence, and supervision
  • Also raises privacy and monitoring concerns

This is the social side of gaze. Eye direction can be both information gathering and a signal to others.

Fatigue Effects

  • Fatigue can change vigilance and scan consistency
  • Blink behavior, fixation duration, and missed events may shift
  • No single gaze measure proves fatigue
  • Interpret fatigue with performance, timing, and task context

Keep the claim cautious. Fatigue is a possible interpretation, not something to infer from one gaze metric alone.

Gaze Bias, Comparison, Confidence

  • People may look longer at options they later choose
  • Repeated comparison can reflect uncertainty or careful checking
  • Faster, cleaner comparison patterns may indicate confidence
  • These patterns require task and outcome data to interpret

Use this as a decision-making bridge: gaze can show comparison behavior, but confidence needs supporting evidence.

Evaluation of a Traffic Control Room

  • Domain: live road traffic management control room.
  • Human role: monitor multiple displays and respond to incidents.
  • HCI issue: operators may use available visual information differently.
  • Measurement idea: mobile eye tracking captures workflow in the field.
  • Figure focus: the workstation layout and Participant 1’s visual sampling.

This case is important because it is a field study in a real control room. The setting is not perfectly controlled, but that is the point. Operators work across multiple displays, with real interruptions, preferences, and routines. Starke and colleagues show that operators differed in visual sampling and task-component weighting. For students, the main lesson is that HCI methods have to work in real environments, not just in clean laboratory tasks. The design question becomes: how should the workspace support both the required workflow and individual operator strategies?

AOI Transitions

This slide shows the experienced-driver AOI transition matrix. Gaze transition entropy, or GTE, summarizes how predictable the next AOI is after the current one. Low GTE means a narrower, repeated transition path; high GTE means more varied movement among AOIs. SGE describes where gaze is distributed; GTE describes the order of movement. Chung and colleagues report higher GTE for experienced drivers, consistent with broader scanning across mirrors and traffic-related AOIs.
