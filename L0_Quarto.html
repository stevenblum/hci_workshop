Section 0.1: Introduction

  • Purpose and workshop frame
  • HCI, human factors, and modeling fields
  • Research venues and reference sources
  • Concepts used across later lessons

Use this section divider as the start of the first vertical stack. The section goal is orientation: students should understand what the workshop is about, how HCI connects to human factors and performance modeling, and what vocabulary they will need for later lessons.

Conceptual Outcomes

  • Human interaction principles: how perception, attention, memory, action, and workload shape technology use.
  • HCI as an engineering discipline: how interfaces can be analyzed, measured, and improved.
  • Task models: how human goals, actions, decisions, and errors can be represented systematically.
  • Visual attention: how gaze behavior reveals what users notice, ignore, revisit, or misunderstand.
  • Cognitive load: how task demands can exceed available mental resources.
  • Human-AI interaction: how automation and AI agents change roles, trust, attention, and decision-making.

Use this slide to frame what students should understand by the end of the workshop. The concepts connect human capabilities and limits to interface analysis, task modeling, visual attention, cognitive load, and human-AI interaction.

Applied Outcomes

  • Decompose tasks: break complex human activity into goals, subtasks, actions, and decision points.
  • Build interaction models: use methods such as HTA, KLM, and GOMS to represent human performance.
  • Collect evidence: gather behavioral, gaze, physiological, and qualitative data from users.
  • Analyze interface demands: identify where an interface creates attention, memory, or workload problems.
  • Validate models: compare predicted behavior with observed user performance.
  • Redesign interfaces: use evidence to improve usability, efficiency, safety, and operator experience.

Use this slide to frame what students should be able to do. The applied outcomes move from decomposing work, to modeling performance, to gathering data, validating predictions, and redesigning interfaces.

Dix, Finlay, Abowd, & Beale, Human–Computer Interaction, 3rd ed., 2004; Helander, Landauer, & Prabhu, Handbook of Human-Computer Interaction, 1997; John & Kieras, “The GOMS Family of Analysis Techniques,” 1994.

Related Fields of Research

  • Human-Computer Interaction: studies how people use and experience interactive systems.
  • Human-centered design: starts design from user goals, needs, and limitations.
  • HMI: focuses on human operation of machines, equipment, and control systems.
  • UX and usability: evaluate whether interfaces are useful, learnable, and efficient.
  • Cognitive architectures: computational models of perception, memory, and action.
  • Human performance modeling: predicts time, errors, workload, and skill demands.
  • Human factors: designs work systems around human capabilities and constraints.
  • Computational psychology: uses formal models to explain human behavior.

This workshop sits at the intersection of several fields. Human-Computer Interaction gives us the broad study of people and interactive systems. Human-centered design and usability give us design practices for improving systems. Human-Machine Interaction and human factors connect the material to machines, control rooms, vehicles, and safety-critical systems. Cognitive architectures and human performance models help us make formal predictions. Computational psychology gives us a way to connect human behavior to models that can be tested.

Dix et al., Human–Computer Interaction, 2004; Helander et al., Handbook of Human-Computer Interaction, 1997; John & Kieras, 1994.

CHI is one of the major conference communities. Depending on the topic, related papers may also appear in interaction design, visualization, intelligent interfaces, CSCW, human factors, ergonomics, and behavior-focused journals. The key idea is that HCI work is spread across computing, engineering, psychology, design, and applied human factors.

We will borrow from the HCI curriculum tradition while also emphasizing task analysis, cognitive modeling, eye tracking, workload measurement, and Industry 4.0 interfaces.

Concepts We Will Use

  • Interface design process: move from user needs to prototypes and evaluation.
  • Design heuristics: practical rules for spotting common usability problems.
  • Task analysis: decompose goals, subtasks, decisions, and information needs.
  • Cognitive architectures: model human perception, memory, learning, and action.
  • Eye tracking: measure fixations, saccades, AOIs, scanpaths, and attention shifts.
  • Mental models: explain how users think a system works and what actions make sense.
  • Cognitive load: estimate the mental demand created by tasks and interfaces.
  • Physiological measures: use biosignals as indirect evidence of workload or attention.

This is the vocabulary students will see throughout the workshop. The design process and heuristics help us create and critique interfaces. Task analysis helps us describe the structure of human work. Cognitive architectures and human performance models help us predict behavior. Eye tracking gives us evidence about visual attention. Mental models help explain user expectations. Cognitive load and physiological measures help us understand how demanding the interaction is. Later, we can connect these measures to machine learning and AI-supported analysis.

Dix et al., 2004; Diaper & Stanton, 2004; John & Kieras, 1994; Land & Tatler, Looking and Acting, 2009.

Section 0.2: Case Studies

  • Driving under divided attention

Use this divider to signal the shift from conceptual orientation to examples. These case studies should help students see that HCI methods apply to industrial systems, transportation, healthcare, and manufacturing work, not only to desktop or consumer software.

Navy Air Defense System Modeling

  • Domain: safety-critical ship systems with high operator responsibility.
  • Human role: monitor complex displays and act under time pressure.
  • HCI issue: workstation complexity creates perceptual and cognitive demand.
  • Modeling idea: cognitive architectures predict task time and training demands.
  • Figure focus: an AEGIS workstation as an operator-centered design problem.

This case is a strong opening example because students can see the complexity immediately. The workstation is not just a screen; it is part of a safety-critical work system. The operator must monitor information, interpret it, decide what matters, and act correctly under pressure. Chipman and Kieras use this kind of setting to motivate operator-centered design and cognitive modeling. The teaching point is that complexity creates measurable demands, and those demands can be modeled before a system is redesigned or deployed.

Navy Air Defense System Modeling

Chipman & Kieras, “Operator Centered Design of Ship Systems,” 2004, Figure 1, p. 2.

Use the workstation image to ask what the operator must perceive, remember, decide, and do. The point is to move students from “this looks complicated” to a more analytic question: which parts of the design create perceptual and cognitive workload?

Refinery Control Room HMI

  • Domain: refinery control room with continuous process monitoring.
  • Human role: detect process deviations and maintain situation awareness.
  • HCI issue: workload changes as alarms, trends, and deviations increase.
  • Measurement idea: scanpaths reveal how operators sample display information.
  • Figure focus: sample scanpaths across a refinery control-room display.

This case shows students how eye tracking can be used in a dynamic monitoring task. In a refinery control room, the operator is not completing a single isolated action. The work is continuous. The operator must keep track of changing conditions and decide where to look next. The scanpath figure is useful because it makes information sampling visible. The HCI question is whether the interface helps operators notice important changes at the right time, or whether it forces inefficient search.

Refinery Control Room HMI

Noah & Rothrock, “Using Eye Tracking for Live Measures of Workload in a Refinery Control Room Process Monitoring Task,” 2015, Figure 2, p. 3.

Use this figure to show that gaze data can make monitoring strategy visible. Ask students what display regions seem to attract attention, and what additional task context they would need before interpreting the scanpath.

Driving Under Divided Attention

  • Domain: simulated highway driving with a sudden swerving-vehicle hazard.
  • Human role: maintain lane control while reading a roadside information board.
  • HCI issue: divided visual attention can delay hazard perception and response.
  • Measurement idea: combine eye tracking, HTA, and GOMS reaction-time modeling.
  • Design use: identify cues for driver-assistance warnings and interface support.

This case connects task analysis directly to transportation safety. Yang, Kim, and Nazareth study a driver who must keep driving while also reading a roadside sign. The HCI issue is not only distraction in general; it is the timing of attention shifts between the roadway, mirrors, speed panel, and sign while a hazard develops.

Frame the case as a bridge between Lesson 0 and later methods. HTA represents the structure of the driving task under divided attention. GOMS then turns parts of that structure into operators and estimated times, using eye-tracking AOIs and task sequences.

Yang, Kim, & Nazareth, “Hierarchical Task Analysis for Driving under Divided Attention,” 2019.

Driving Under Divided Attention: Event

Yang, Kim, & Nazareth, “Hierarchical Task Analysis for Driving under Divided Attention,” 2019, Figure 1, p. 2.

Use this figure to explain the scenario. The participant drives in the right lane while a front-left event vehicle is triggered and swerves into the driver’s lane. At the same time, the participant must read and say the information on the red roadside board.

The event begins when the participant-controlled vehicle reaches the trigger location and the event vehicle starts moving. The event ends when the event vehicle completes the swerving action. This gives the study a defined interval for comparing crash and non-crash reaction behavior.

Driving Under Divided Attention: HTA

Yang, Kim, & Nazareth, “Hierarchical Task Analysis for Driving under Divided Attention,” 2019, Figure 2, p. 3.

This figure decomposes the overall driving task into perception, cognition, task transition, red-board performance, decision, and motor response. Point out that the red-board task is not separate from driving; it competes with driving for visual and cognitive resources.

Use the highlighted parts to discuss crash versus non-crash differences. The paper reports that non-crash drivers tended to check the speed panel and had shorter transitions between the front view and the red-board task.

Use this table to show how eye-tracking AOIs become GOMS operators with time estimates. The authors used AOI visit times to estimate several visual-check operators, then used the operator sequence to compare reaction time in crash and non-crash cases.

The ANOVA table shows that transition time between the driving task and the red-board task was significantly longer for crash cases than non-crash cases. Monitoring surrounding and red-board task duration were not significant in this comparison.

The main teaching point is that task analysis, gaze data, and GOMS can be linked: HTA provides the structure, eye tracking provides observed timing, and GOMS supports comparison of cognitive-perceptual paths.

CNC Interface Evaluation

  • Domain: CNC machine control panels used in industrial manufacturing.
  • Human role: operate machine functions through panel layout and controls.
  • HCI issue: poor HMI design can increase burden and slow operation.
  • Measurement idea: combine physiology, eye tracking, behavior, and ratings.
  • Design use: compare interface schemes and identify elements to improve.
  • Figure focus: four alternative CNC control-panel HMI schemes.

This case connects the workshop directly to Industry 4.0. A CNC control panel affects both efficiency and user experience. Dou and colleagues evaluated multiple interface schemes using synchronized data sources. That is the key teaching point: a good evaluation does not depend on one number. Task behavior, gaze, physiology, subjective ratings, and statistical summaries can validate one another. This gives designers more confidence when choosing a design or targeting specific interface elements for improvement.

CNC Interface Evaluation

Dou, Zhang, Zhao, Pei, & Qin, “Human-Machine Interface Evaluation of CNC Machine Control Panel through Multidimensional Experimental Data Synchronous Testing Analysis Method,” 2017, Figure 2, p. 1198.

Use the four schemes to discuss comparative evaluation. Students should notice that the design question is not simply whether one panel is attractive; it is whether the layout supports efficient and low-burden operation.

Visual Strategies for Reading Blueprints

  • Domain: mechanical part drawings used in manufacturing and maintenance.
  • Human role: interpret 2D drawings to understand part geometry and production.
  • HCI issue: expert visual strategies are structured but often tacit.
  • Method idea: eye tracking plus retrospective think-aloud explains strategy.
  • Figure focus: gaze heatmaps translated into a hierarchical task analysis.

Blueprint reading is a useful example because the task can look simple to a novice. The expert is not merely looking at the drawing; the expert is using a strategy. Nambiar and colleagues combine eye tracking with retrospective think-aloud to understand what the expert looks at and why. They then translate that strategy into a hierarchical task analysis. This is a nice bridge between visual attention and task modeling. It also shows how hidden expertise can become teachable.

Visual Strategies for Reading Blueprints

Nambiar et al., “Understanding the Visualization Strategies used by Experts when Reading Mechanical Part Drawings using Eye Tracking,” 2013, Figure 4, p. 507.

Use the heatmap to ask what is visible and what is still missing. Eye tracking shows where attention was directed, but retrospective explanation is needed to understand the strategy behind those fixations.

Visual Strategies for Reading Blueprints

Nambiar et al., “Understanding the Visualization Strategies used by Experts when Reading Mechanical Part Drawings using Eye Tracking,” 2013, Figure 5, p. 509.

This slide makes the bridge from gaze evidence to task modeling explicit. The expert’s visual behavior becomes an analyzable procedure that can be taught, compared, or redesigned around.

Evaluation of a Traffic Control Room

  • Domain: live road traffic management control room.
  • Human role: monitor multiple displays and respond to incidents.
  • HCI issue: operators may use available visual information differently.
  • Measurement idea: mobile eye tracking captures workflow in the field.
  • Figure focus: the workstation layout and Participant 1’s visual sampling.

This case is important because it is a field study in a real control room. The setting is not perfectly controlled, but that is the point. Operators work across multiple displays, with real interruptions, preferences, and routines. Starke and colleagues show that operators differed in visual sampling and task-component weighting. For students, the main lesson is that HCI methods have to work in real environments, not just in clean laboratory tasks. The design question becomes: how should the workspace support both the required workflow and individual operator strategies?

Evaluation of a Traffic Control Room

Starke, Baber, Cooke, & Howes, “Workflows and Individual Differences during Visually Guided Routine Tasks in a Road Traffic Management Control Room,” 2017, Figure 1, p. 81.

Use this image to ground the field setting. The workstation is a real work environment with multiple displays, physical layout constraints, and operational routines that shape visual behavior.

Visual Strategies of Surgeons

  • Domain: simulated surgical training with expert and novice learners.
  • Human role: perform procedural tasks while monitoring task-relevant areas.
  • HCI issue: novice attention is often more diffuse than expert attention.
  • Measurement idea: compare fixations, saccades, blinks, and gaze distribution.
  • Figure focus: the simulation setup and novices’ most frequent scanpaths.

This final case connects eye tracking to expertise and training. Li and colleagues compare experts and novices during simulated surgical training. The goal is not just to show that experts perform better. The goal is to understand how their visual attention differs. Experts tend to focus more on task-relevant areas, while novice attention can be more spread out. That opens the door to training systems that detect novice-like gaze behavior and provide feedback, prompts, or expert gaze examples.

Visual Strategies of Surgeons

Li et al., “Using Eye Tracking to Examine Expert-Novice Differences during Simulated Surgical Training: A Case Study,” 2023, Figure 2, p. 4.

Use this setup figure to discuss measurement context. The apparatus and task setting matter because they influence what gaze can reveal and what performance constraints participants experience.
